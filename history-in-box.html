Aboriginal Star Maps: Ancient Navigation Systems

Star Maps

Aboriginal star maps are patterns of stars used as memory aids for navigation. Unlike regular maps, these star patterns don't directly show the direction of travel. Instead, they represent important waypoints along a journey route, similar to how modern GPS systems use waypoints to mark stopping or turning points.

The stars in these patterns correspond to significant locations on the landscape, such as waterholes for drinking water, places to change direction, and rest stops along the route.

Stars to Songlines

For Kamilaroi and Euahlayi peoples in northwest NSW, when the summer travel was being planned in August or September, a person who had travelled the intended route was tasked with teaching others, who had not made this journey, how to navigate to the intended destination.

The pattern of stars, known as the star map, was taught and memorised using songs, known as songlines.

Songlines are cultural stories that travel across the landscape, connecting places through song and navigation knowledge. Aboriginal people say that the landscape itself holds these songs.

Songlines for Navigation

Songlines cover impressive distances. Kamilaroi people have local songlines from Goodooga that extend up to 700 kilometres away. However, long-distance routes stretch over 1,500 kilometres, such as routes from Alice Springs to meet Euahlayi people for ceremonies.

Star maps were used by the Kamilaroi people to navigate from Goodooga to the Bunya Mountains (northwest of Brisbane), where the Aboriginal Bunya nut festival was held every three years until disrupted by European invasion.

Connection to Modern Roads

Research reveals that many ancient Aboriginal travel routes align closely with today's major highways. This occurred because early European explorers used Aboriginal guides, and local Aboriginal people who likely gave them directions of established routes.

These routes would have been the easiest paths across the landscape, so settlers and drovers continued using these same paths. Eventually, the paths became modern roads and highways.

The Kamilaroi Highway in New South Wales is one example where this Aboriginal contribution is officially recognised in the road's name.
